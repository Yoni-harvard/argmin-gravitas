Don't know higher maths, don't know code

Hell mode.
If you're like me, you'll need qualitative meaning first, to get you fired up: Gleick's The Information and Nilsson's Quest for Artificial Intelligence, Hofstadter's Goedel, Escher, Bach, Floridi's Blackwell Guide to the Philosophy of Computation, Howson's , O'Neil's Weapons of Math Destruction, Domingos' Master Algorithm. Then the real work begins:
  • Start with Python: Zed Shaw's Learn the Hard Way will get you moving, if you do as he tells you.
  • You must read other people's code to improve. Try a gamified site like CodeWars, which shows you elegant solutions only after you sweat out a dreadful hack.
  • Once you are comfortable with functions, work through Think Stats.
  • I enjoyed Statistical Inference for Everyone

Know higher maths, can't code

Grasp ten times reach.
  • Learn the Hard Way might be too slow.
  • Tom Mitchell's Machine Learning is old but very clear.
  • O'Neil's Doing Data Science is chatty but gives decently involved models in R.
  • Neural Networks and Deep Learning is a great read.
  • Jaynes' unfinished masterwork, The Logic of Science will tie together acres of mathematical ground. Start by reading his bitchy footnotes.
  • Ron Rivest's Intro to Algos.

  • Pearl's Causality
